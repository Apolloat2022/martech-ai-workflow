# Bottleneck & Redundancy Register

> Fictional reference scenario authored as an architecture portfolio piece. Not a record of a client engagement.

**Audience:** Marketing operations leadership, process owners, and anyone about to greenlight an AI use case against this process.

## How to read this register

Each row is either a **queue bottleneck** — a specific handoff (per [D1](01-current-state-process-map.md)) where elapsed time accumulates — or a **systemic/judgment constraint** — a root cause that isn't itself a queue but explains why an adjacent queue is as deep as it is. The two are marked separately because they take different fixes: a queue bottleneck can sometimes be shortened by better tooling; a judgment constraint usually can't, and treating it like a tooling problem is how AI projects end up automating the wrong layer.

Cycle-time costs are `[illustrative]` per-occurrence estimates, not strictly additive — several run in parallel across the four localization markets, and some overlap with baseline "in work" time rather than sitting purely in queue.

## Register

| ID | Handoff ref | Symptom | Root cause | Cycle-time cost | Automation candidacy | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| B-01 | H4 → H5 | Claims issues are caught only after brand has already signed off, forcing a full rework loop back through creative rather than a copy-only fix | No claims checklist is applied before brand review; brand has no visibility into what legal will flag | +4 business days `[illustrative]` per occurrence | **High** | Unsubstantiated-claim patterns (superlatives, health/efficacy claims, comparative claims) are pattern-matchable and stable enough for an LLM-assisted pre-screen at the creative-development stage, before brand ever sees the asset. This doesn't replace legal's sign-off (see B-10) — it moves the catch earlier so the rework loop that does happen is smaller. |
| B-02 | H6 → H7 → H8 | Localized copy frequently fails claims review a second time, per market, because translators work from translated copy with no claims guidance attached | No shared, market-localized claims taxonomy travels with the asset into localization; the constraint that mattered in English isn't re-stated in the brief the translator receives | +6 business days `[illustrative]`, compounding across up to 4 markets | **Medium** | An LLM-assisted check — flagging claims-bearing phrases in the localized copy and doing a back-translation consistency pass before submission — can catch a meaningful share of repeat failures. It's Medium, not High, because market-specific ad law (e.g. what counts as a comparative claim) is jurisdiction-dependent and the tool can flag, not clear. |
| B-03 | H2 → H3 | Brand review outcomes are inconsistent creative-to-creative depending on which reviewer is assigned, driving avoidable rework | Brand guidelines exist mainly as a slide deck, not a queryable reference the agency can check against while drafting | +2 business days `[illustrative]` per occurrence | **Medium** | A retrieval-backed brand-guideline assistant, surfaced to the agency *during* drafting rather than at review time, reduces guideline-miss rework. It doesn't fix reviewer inconsistency itself (see B-09) — that's a calibration problem, not a lookup problem. |
| B-04 | H1 | Briefs frequently arrive missing required inputs (claims substantiation source, target market list), and the agency pauses to request clarification | Brief intake has no structured, enforced template | +3 business days `[illustrative]` per occurrence | **High** | A structured brief-intake form with required-field validation is mechanical, low-risk, and doesn't involve any judgment call an AI system would need to make — arguably barely needs AI at all, just a form. Flagged High because it's cheap and unambiguous, not because it's exciting. |
| B-05 | H9 | Localized asset variants are manually re-tagged and renamed per market before DAM ingestion, four times per asset | No shared metadata taxonomy or naming convention is enforced at the localization → DAM handoff | +1.5 business days `[illustrative]` per asset | **High** | Metadata templating from the source asset's tags plus the market code is mechanical transformation, not judgment. Strong automation candidate and a natural fit for the ingestion pipeline in [D5](05-data-pipelines.md). |
| B-06 | H10, H11 | Cross-channel activation timing (email, web, paid social, in-store) is sequenced manually over email threads | No shared activation calendar or orchestration layer across channel owners | +2 business days `[illustrative]` coordination overhead per cycle | **Medium** | Scheduling and dependency tracking can be tooled. Sequencing judgment — what launches first, embargo handling, which market waits for which — stays with a human channel owner; this is assistive, not autonomous. |
| B-07 | H12, H13 → readout | Performance data from the CRM/lifecycle platform and regional/in-store systems arrives in incompatible formats; Product Marketing manually reconciles it into one readout | No shared analytics schema across the CDP, CRM, and regional reporting tools (see [D3](03-data-landscape.md)) | +3 business days `[illustrative]` per cycle | **High** | This is ETL and schema reconciliation — mechanical, well-scoped, and exactly the kind of pipeline work D5 targets. High confidence, low judgment risk. |
| B-08 | Underlies H4→H5, H6→H8 | The claims-review queue is deep regardless of how well-prepared assets arrive, because a small, centralized Legal & Compliance team reviews all four markets serially with no risk-based triage | Understaffed shared service with flat, non-tiered intake — every asset gets the same review depth regardless of actual risk | n/a — systemic capacity constraint; its cost is embedded in the B-01/B-02 estimates above, not separately additive | **None** | This is a staffing and triage-policy problem, not an AI problem. An LLM pre-screen (B-01, B-02) can shrink what reaches the queue, but the queue's depth is fundamentally a headcount and prioritization decision. Recommending "add an AI reviewer" here would be recommending automation of a resourcing gap — the honest answer is a staffing/triage-policy ask, not a build. |
| B-09 | Underlies H2→H3 | Brand review verdicts vary by which reviewer is assigned — the same creative can pass or fail depending on who reviews it | No calibration rubric across reviewers; judgment is individual, not standardized | n/a — its cost shows up in B-03 above, not separately | **None** | Brand fit and creative quality are exactly the kind of high-context, subjective judgment where AI assistance either rubber-stamps existing bias or oversimplifies taste into a checklist. The fix is a calibration rubric and reviewer rotation — a process and management fix, not a technology one. |
| B-10 | H4, H7 (the review itself, not the queue around it) | N/A — this row exists to state explicitly what is *not* being proposed for automation | The claims sign-off is a regulated judgment call with legal accountability attached, not a classification task | n/a — this is the judgment step itself | **None** | An LLM can flag candidate issues before a reviewer sees them (B-01, B-02) — it must not make the sign-off decision. Full automation here creates unmanaged regulatory and brand-safety exposure no model-quality improvement removes, because the exposure is about *who is accountable*, not about accuracy. This is the constraint [D10](10-governance.md)'s human-in-the-loop policy is built around, and the reference slice's policy-check step is deliberately designed to route to a human here rather than auto-approve. |
| B-11 | Interior of H1→H2 (creative development itself) | N/A — general observation, not a delay symptom | Generative tooling could draft creative directly, bypassing the agency | n/a | **Low** | Assistive use (ideation support, first-draft variants) is reasonable; wholesale automation of creative development would undercut the differentiated capability MRG retains the agency for. Low, not None, because *some* assistive use is defensible — but it is not where this process's real cost sits, and it is not recommended as a priority. |

## What this changes

D4's target architecture is only permitted to design automation for handoffs scored **High** or **Medium** here (B-01, B-02, B-03, B-04, B-05, B-06, B-07). The three **None** rows (B-08, B-09, B-10) are a fence, not an oversight — they tell a reviewer, and they told the author, where an AI recommendation would have been the easy, wrong answer. Any future proposal to automate claims sign-off, brand-review verdicts, or legal staffing should be checked against this register before it's approved, and should carry the burden of explaining what changed since this assessment.
